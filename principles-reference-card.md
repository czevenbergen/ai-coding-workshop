# Building with AI — Principles Reference Card

**4 principles for effective AI-assisted coding**

---

### 1. AI as Collaborator

AI is a thinking partner from ideation through execution. Use it to brainstorm, design, build, and debug — but you stay in the driver's seat. The AI suggests; you decide.

### 2. Work in Small Steps

One instruction at a time. Plan what you need, execute it, review the result, then move on. Large tasks should be broken into small, independently completable pieces. "Build a stakeholder directory" is too big. "Add a route that lists all stakeholders" is the right size. Resist the urge to ask for everything at once — small requests are easier to verify, easier to fix, and produce better results.

### 3. Context Shapes Output

The AI only knows what you tell it and what it can see. When the AI gives a strange answer, the first question is: "Does it have enough context?" Specific prompts beat vague ones — "Add a delete button next to each stakeholder in the table" beats "improve the app." Good project structure and conventions compound into better output. Persistent instructions (like a copilot instructions file) mean you don't have to repeat yourself.

### 4. Verify, Don't Trust

Read everything the AI gives you. Don't just check if the code runs — understand the structure. You'll spend more time reading and evaluating AI output than writing prompts. That's normal — it's the job. AI fails in predictable ways. Learn to spot them:

- **Hallucination** — invents APIs, libraries, or patterns that don't exist
- **Over-engineering** — adds unnecessary complexity you didn't ask for
- **Drift** — gradually deviates from your intent over long conversations

When you spot these, step back. A fresh conversation with a specific prompt is often faster than trying to fix a derailed one.
